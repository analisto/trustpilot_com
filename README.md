# Trustpilot Business Intelligence Report

- **Dataset:** 182,699 unique businesses across 21 industry sectors and 163 sub-categories
- **Source:** Trustpilot category pages — public business listings
- **Published dataset:** [Kaggle — Trustpilot](https://www.kaggle.com/datasets/ismetsemedov/trustpilot)

---

## Executive Summary

This report analyzes the competitive trust landscape across Trustpilot's entire platform. Three findings stand out above all others:

1. **Most businesses are invisible.** 57,363 businesses (31%) have zero reviews — they exist on Trustpilot but have built no social proof whatsoever.
2. **The majority of rated businesses are underperforming.** Over half of active businesses score below 3.5 out of 5, placing them in the "Average" or lower tier.
3. **A small group of review leaders dominates.** The top 0.5% of businesses (by review count) have accumulated review volumes that dwarf the rest of the market — creating enormous competitive moats.

### Actions to consider
- Businesses sitting at 4.3 or 4.4 should treat reaching 4.5 as a critical business objective — the visibility uplift from being "Recommended" can materially affect traffic and conversion.

## Chart 7 — High-Trust Businesses Attract Dramatically More Reviews

![Review Volume vs Trust Score](charts/07_review_volume_vs_trust_score.png)

### What the chart shows
The average and median number of reviews for businesses in each trust score band. Both measures rise sharply as trust scores increase.

### Why it matters
This chart reveals a powerful **virtuous cycle**: businesses that earn high trust scores attract more reviews, which in turn reinforces their high scores and visibility. The Excellent tier (4.5–5.0) averages 1,501 reviews per business — ten times the average of the Poor tier (2.0–2.9).

The median tells an equally important story: even within the Good tier (4.0–4.4), the median business has only 9 reviews, while the average is 605 — meaning a small number of review leaders massively skew the average upward. The market is not evenly distributed; it is winner-takes-most.

### Actions to consider
- Businesses that invest in customer experience early create compounding review advantages. Early movers in review collection build leads that become increasingly difficult for competitors to close.
- The gap between median and average across all tiers signals that a handful of businesses dominate each category. Understanding who these leaders are and what they do differently is a worthwhile strategic exercise.
